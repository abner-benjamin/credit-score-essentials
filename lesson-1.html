What is a Credit Score?

A credit score is a three-digit number ranging from 300 to 850 that represents your creditworthiness — essentially, how risky or safe you are to lend money to. It’s like a trust score for your financial behavior.

If you've ever heard someone say “my credit is good” or “I can't get approved because of my credit,” this is what they’re talking about. A good credit score signals to lenders that you're likely to pay back money you borrow.

Getting approved for credit cards or loans

Why it matters

A higher score gives you better access and lower interest rates.

Renting an apartment

Landlords often check credit to see if you’re reliable with bills.

Employment screening

Some employers, especially in finance or security, check credit reports (not scores) as part of background checks.

Getting a car loan

Lower credit means higher interest, which means you pay more over time.

Example

Imagine two people apply for a $10,000 car loan. One has excellent credit and gets an interest rate of 5%. The other has poor credit and gets 15%. Over five years, the second person pays over $1,500 more in interest — all because of their credit score.
